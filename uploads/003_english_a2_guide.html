Comprehensive A2 English Study Guide: Unlock 2 (Units 5–8)

This guide provides a high-level pedagogical synthesis of the Unlock 2 curriculum, designed to move learners from foundational description toward complex academic argumentation. As an EFL Pedagogical Content Specialist, I have structured this document to highlight the linguistic progressions and critical thinking skills required for academic success at the A2 level.

1. Unit 5: Sports and Competition

Strategic Context

Unit 5 represents a pivotal shift in the learner’s journey, transitioning from the description of static places to the description of dynamic actions and processes. By exploring the context of sports science—from the Sydney Triathlon to the grit of the Tough Guy race—learners move beyond simple identification to explaining how movement occurs and how sequences unfold over time.

Grammar: Prepositions of Movement & Subject-Verb Agreement

To describe the mechanics of dynamic action, learners must master how objects move through three-dimensional space.

Prepositions of Movement

PrepositionUsage in Sports ContextExample
AcrossMoving from one side to another.The athletes ran across the finish line.
ThroughMoving within a 3D space or obstacle.The racer crawled through the mud in the Tough Guy race.
IntoEntering a container or specific area.The diver jumped into the water.

Subject-Verb Agreement

Academic precision requires a strict relationship between the subject and the verb.

  • Singular Subjects: Require a singular verb.
  • Example: The 96-year-old bungee jumper leaps from the platform.
  • Plural Subjects: Require a base verb.
  • Example: Participants climb over high walls.
  • Key Rule: Agreement is vital for keeping descriptions of complex processes clear for the reader.

Vocabulary and Key Themes

Vocabulary to Describe Sport

Reading Skill: Scanning to Predict Content

Learners practice looking for specific data (numbers, dates, or proper nouns) to anticipate a text's theme before a full reading.

🔍 Pedagogical Insight: The "So What?"

The strategic importance of ordering events in a process allows a learner to move from producing isolated, simple sentences to a cohesive narrative flow. This logic is not just about sports; it is the foundation for explaining any technical system or chronological event in higher-level academic writing.

Writing and Speaking Focus: The Process Paragraph

Use this checklist when writing about a process, such as the Sydney Triathlon:

  • [ ] Write a topic sentence identifying the process.
  • [ ] Use sequence markers (First, Next, Finally).
  • [ ] Remove unrelated information: Delete any sentence that does not directly contribute to the specific steps of the process to maintain academic focus.

Unit Summary & Transition

Unit 5 builds the ability to describe movement and physical sequences. This command of "process" language is essential as we move to Unit 6, where these skills are applied to the historical trajectories of the professional world.

2. Unit 6: Business

Strategic Context

Unit 6 applies language skills to professional environments and the historical evolution of corporations. By examining the Story of Google alongside Human Resources themes in the World of Work, learners practice using English to bridge the gap between a company’s humble beginnings and its modern global operations.

Grammar: Present/Past Simple & Time Clauses

Professional communication requires a clear contrast between a company's history and its current status.

Present Simple vs. Past Simple

TenseFunctionExample
Past SimpleDescribes completed historical facts.Google started in a small garage in 1998.
Present SimpleDescribes current status and operations.Amazon operates massive fulfillment centres today.

Time Clauses with When

Use when to anchor past events to specific timeframes.

  • Pattern: When + [Past Event], [Resulting State]
  • Example: "When Google started, it was a small company."

Vocabulary and Business Collocations

Business Collocations

  • Start a business
  • Run a company
  • World of work
  • Fulfillment centre

Reading Skills

  • Annotating a text: Taking notes in margins to isolate key business dates.
  • Skimming: Reading quickly to identify the "big picture" of a company's success.

🔍 Pedagogical Insight: The "So What?"

Adding details to main facts (such as specific dates, percentages, or locations) is critical for professional communication. It transforms a generic statement into a credible, evidence-based claim, which is a hallmark of business-level English.

Writing Task: The Business Narrative

When writing the history of a business, organize the paragraph in time order:

  1. Founding: Use the Past Simple to describe the start.
  2. Growth: Use time clauses to explain key milestones.
  3. Current State: Use the Present Simple to describe the business today.

Unit Summary & Transition

This unit focused on the history and operations of organizations. Having mastered the evolution of groups, Unit 7 shifts the focus to the individual human element: the character traits that define a leader.

3. Unit 7: People

Strategic Context

Unit 7 shifts the pedagogical lens to sociology, evaluating the specific character traits that define Incredible People. Learners move from corporate histories to the evaluation of role models, using specialized linguistic tools to define what makes a person significant.

Grammar: Noun Phrases & Modals of Necessity

Noun Phrases with of

This structure allows learners to define abstract concepts or titles.

  • Structure: The [Noun] of a [Noun]
  • Example: "The qualities of a leader."
  • Context Note: In this unit's video, we see this in "The life of a gold prospector."

Modals of Necessity

To define role models, we must express requirements using must and have to.

  • "A role model must be brave."
  • "A successful person has to work hard."

Vocabulary: Descriptive Adjectives

Adjectives for People

Reading Skill: Categorizing Ideas

Managing descriptive data by grouping it into themes (e.g., Personal Qualities vs. Achievements).

🔍 Pedagogical Insight: The "So What?"

The concluding sentence of a paragraph is more than a summary; it is a tool for reinforcement. A strong conclusion solidifies the author's opinion on a person's character, ensuring the reader is persuaded by the analysis rather than just informed of the facts.

Writing Task: Character Analysis

Students write an explanatory paragraph about a role model. The focus is on identifying a specific individual and using descriptive adjectives and modals of necessity to justify their status as a role model.

Unit Summary & Transition

4. Unit 8: The Universe

Strategic Context

The final unit focuses on Space Science, pushing learners toward complex language to support arguments regarding Commercial space travel and Life on other planets. The objective is to synthesize all previous skills into formal academic argumentation.

Grammar: Complex Sentences & Infinitives of Purpose

Complex Sentences with That Clauses

Used to introduce opinions or scientific evidence.

  • Example: "Scientists believe that humans can live on Mars."

Infinitives of Purpose vs. Connectors

It is vital to distinguish between purpose (why we do something) and reason (the cause).

  • Infinitive of Purpose (To + Verb):

"Governments spend money to explore planets." (Shows the goal.)

  • Connectors (Because/So):

"They explore space because it is important." (Shows the reason.)

"Space is far, so the trip is long." (Shows the result.)

Vocabulary: Argumentation & Evidence

Academic Support Phrases

  • According to...
  • Research shows...
  • For example...

Reading Skills

Identifying the Author’s Purpose

Determining if a text is meant to inform or persuade.

Distinguishing Fact from Opinion

  • Fact: "Mars is the fourth planet from the sun."
  • Opinion: "We should spend more money to live on Mars."

🔍 Pedagogical Insight: The "So What?"

Essay Organization (Introduction, Body, and Conclusion) is the ultimate hallmark of academic success. High-level topics like space exploration require a structured argument to ensure that complex evidence remains clear and persuasive to the reader.

Writing Task: The Opinion Essay

Learners draft an opinion essay on government funding for space.

Mandatory Components

  1. A clear opinion statement (Topic sentence).
  2. Supporting reasons backed by evidence/facts.
  3. A conclusion that reinforces the stance.
